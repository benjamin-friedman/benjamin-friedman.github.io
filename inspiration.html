Hans zimmer is the greatest composer of the modern era and one of the greatest composers of all time. The breadth and depth of his work is unrivaled having composed some of the most beautiful, powerful, and emotional music ever written in the soundtracks of many major motion pictures. Some of his best work comes from The Dark Knight trilogy, The Pirates Of The Caribbean series, Inception, and Interstellar amongst many other films. From The Dark Knight trilogy his best compositions include Corynorhinus, Myotis, Molossus, A Dark Knight, Agent Of Chaos, and Why Do We Fall. One of the most beautiful scores ever composed is Marry Me from Pirates Of The Caribbean: At World's End in which it was an unreleased track with elements of it being in other tracks. Other incredible songs he has composed include Time from Inception, First Steps, No Time For Caution, and Mountains from Interstellar, and Final Ascent from No Time To Die. A cellist named Stjepan Hauser did an incredible cello rendition of his composition Now We Are Free from Gladiator. Many of the cello tracks here are from Hauser who is known for both his interpretations of traditional classical cello songs and renditions of songs not originally made for the cello. It should be noted that Hans collaborated with James Newton Howard in the first two films in The Dark Knight Trilogy, Klaus Badelt in the first Pirates of the Caribbean film, and Klaus Badelt once again along with Lisa Gerrard in Gladiator so the credit goes to them as well.

Frederic Chopin is one of the greatest classical piano composers of all time. It is not said the he is the greatest because the pursuit of such a choice proves to be untenable. When other composers have produced piano songs so remarkable for which it would be a crime to live without, then saying any single one of them is the greatest feels like a betrayal of the others. How could it be said that Chopin is the greatest when there exists the works of Franz Liszt, Beethoven's forever immortal Moonlight Sonata, and many others. After ample time passes grappling with this impossible and futile decision, the only moral conclusion that can be reached that does not diminish any one composer is that there are a number of composers who are tied for first. However, if an answer to that question were to be compelled, then Chopin would most certainly be one of the strongest candidates. What makes Chopin such an extraordinary piano composer is the quantity of incredible songs he composed. He, like other composers but especially he, had this distinct ability to write piano pieces that captured the soul and heart of man; to take emotion and put it into music. In that sense, Chopin and Hans Zimmer are the same because their genius is most readily apparent by observing that not once or twice but over and over again they composed some of the most remarkable, beautiful, and emotional music ever written. His series of 21 nocturnes perhaps embody this most aptly. Nocturne 19 In E Minor and Nocturne 20 In C Sharp Minor stand out as two of the best amongst them all that most adequately capture the soul and heart of man. Then in Nocturne 1 in B Flat Minor there is the way it goes from A, down to A flat, down to G flat, back up to A flat, and finally back up to A. Moving on to Nocturne 4 In F Major there is the striking contrast between the peace and calm of the first half and the intensity and velocity of the second half. Nocturne 21 In C Minor posseses some of the same essence of Nocturnes 19 and 20, and then after all of that there are still 17 nocturnes remaining each of which is a standalone work of art. Unfortunately, Nocturnes 20 and 21 were not appreciated until after his death due to being published posthumously. This is especially regretful for his 20th nocturne as it is arguably his most famous and undoubtedly one of the most beautiful he ever composed. In addition to his nocturnes his work includes 27 preludes (one of them unfinished), 27 etudes, 3 piano sonatas, and 4 ballades. If the emotion and depth of his nocturnes were not enough then rivaling them are two of his preludes: Prelude 4 In E Minor and Prelude 15 In D Flat Major also known as Raindrop Prelude. It is difficult for words to suffice any worthy articulation regarding Prelude In E Minor, and by his own request Chopin had this song played at his funeral. What makes Prelude In D Flat Major so remarkable is that the first part of the song, albeit nice, is actually rather unremarkable, and then out of nowhere it suddenly pivots into one of the deepest pieces of music ever written starting with its transition to some lower octave notes. Both of these preludes are comparable to Nocturnes 20 and 21 as two of the most beautiful songs ever composed. Then there are songs like Fantaisie Impromptu, Winter Wind, and Revolutionary Etude which capture the intensity and even at times a kind of violence that Chopin is capable of. Finally, the beauty, emotion, power, and technical prowess of his first and most famous Ballade, Ballade No. 1 In G Minor, embodies all of Chopin's qualities and capabilities combining them into one song forming one of the greatest piano songs ever composed. This ballade of his is so powerful and remarkable that it became known as "La Ballade" or "The Ballade" as if to say it is The Ballade of all ballades. Chopin died fairly young at the age of 39. Somewhere out there in the universe are all of the compositions he would have written yet to be heard or experienced.

Franz Schubert is most well known for Serenade but has other incredible compositions as well such as Impromptu In G Flat Major and Piano Trio No. 2 In E Flat Major 2nd Movement. The cello part of Piano Trio No. 2 In E Flat Major 2nd Movement is one of the most emotional cello scores ever written only enhanced by its pairing with the piano forming a musical duality as is always the case when the piano and cello unite. Schubert died even younger than Chopin at the age of 31.

Franz Liszt, like Chopin, is known for his beautiful, elegant, and technically demanding piano pieces such as the Liebestraum No. 3, Consolation No. 3, Romance "O Pourquoi Donc" In E Minor, and his piano arrangement of Paganini's violin composition La Campanella, amongst others. The unique beauty and emotion of Liebestraum No. 3 is unmatched by any other piano song, and the beauty and emotion of Consolation No. 3 and Romance "O Pourqoi Donc" parallels that of Chopin's work. He also did a piano arrangement of Schubert's Serenade which was originally written for strings, and he was able to capture the same emotion and beauty of that song on the piano as Schubert was on the strings.

Ludwig van Beethoven is most well known for his symphonies and his Piano Sonata No. 14 in C Sharp Minor more commonly known as Moonlight Sonata. The sentiment of Moonlight Sonata 1st Movement cannot be underscored enough, and it is a timeless piece that exists in its own realm. Fewer know of Moonlight Sonata 3rd Movement which is regarded as one of the most technically challenging piano pieces ever. Like Chopin's "La Ballade", Moonlight Sonata 3rd Movement revealed unseen heights, dimensions, and possibilities within the piano that were hidden from the world prior to its composition. The fact that Beethoven was able to compose some of his music despite becoming deaf is a testament to how much of a musical genius he sincerely was.

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart's best work includes Fantasia In D Minor, Serenade For Winds 3rd Movement, and The Requiem In D Minor. Mozart died before he could complete The Requiem, and as a result of this there is unfortunately much controversy surrounding the piece and the events leading up to his death. There is debate concerning which parts of The Requiem are his own work, and whether or not his death was the result of a rather sudden illness or a slow deterioration of his health in the months leading up to it. It is also alleged that when he knew he was dying he professed that he believed he was writing The Requiem for himself but that is debated as well. Although it is all speculative, this alleged profession and these facts makes the piece all the more compelling because what is true is that Mozart was struck with an illness at some point during the piece's composition, and he ultimately died before its completion. So, in that sense he was writing The Requiem for himself. The point being, regardless of which parts of The Requiem truly are his own work, regardless of whether or not his illness came on suddenly or it was slow and progressive, and regardless of whether or not he actually uttered that statement or at a minimum believed it in his own conscience, the compellingness of the circumstances is not lessened by the ambiguity surrounding what is historically accurate. I fear that I am writing a Requiem for myself he is alleged to have said.

Johann Sebastian Bach's best work includes Toccata and Fugue In D Minor, Air On The G String, and his famous Prelude from Cello Suite No. 1 In G Major. The almost divine transcendence of Toccata and Fugue In D Minor separates it from every other piece ever written for the organ. Air On The G String, a modified arrangement of Bach's Orchestral Suite No. 3 In D Major 2nd Movement by August Wilhelmj, makes a worthy attempt to embody the music of the air most notably felt in those beautiful, drawn out notes that gracefully hover in the atmosphere. Yo-Yo Ma most exceptionally describes Bach's famed cello prelude as a representation of the infinitude of the natural world. The meaning of this statement is an exquisite fusion of the numerous ways in which the natural world is infinite. It is infinite in that it has infinite beauty: the majestically breathtaking view from a mountaintop, the deep blue of the ocean, the profound essence of an animal derived from millions of years of evolution, or the protective instinct motivated by love. It is infinite in that it is constant and unchanging: the flow of water in a river, the rise and fall of the sun, the unrelenting battle to maintain a balance between opposites, or the inevitability of time and the cycle of life and death. It is infinite in that it is infinite: the infinite number of stars, solar systems, and galaxies, the infinite number of universes perhaps, the infinite number of experiences, and the infinity of consciousness.

Tomaso Albinoni is most well known for his piece Adagio In G Minor also known as "Albinoni's Adagio". The plucking of the background strings serves to propel forward the beauty of the melody by acting somewhat as the heartbeat of the song. Camille Saint-Saens is most well known for his piece The Swan from The Carnival of the Animals. Albinoni's Adagio and The Swan are two of the most beautiful compositions ever written. The Swan is traditionally played by a cello accompanied by either a harp or piano. Neither version is superior but rather they are just different. Thus, The Swan becomes even further remarkable because it is a single song out of which comes two different experiences which are each uniquely beautiful in their own way.

Erik Satie composed a series of Gnossiennes and Gymnopedies with the first composition in each series being the best and most well known. There is a deep level of calmness within his first Gymnopedie, and his first Gnossienne is a hauntingly beautiful piece for which it is difficult to pinpoint which emotion it strikes at. The essence of these compositions truly situates them uniquely amongst all other pieces of music ever composed.

Most would probably recognize Claude Debussy's Claire de lune but fewer have probably heard Reverie or Arabesque No. 1. Claire de lune and Reverie both capture a kind of soothing and tranquil beauty. The true magnificence of Clair de lune lies within the fact that Debussy is able to maintain this even in the parts where the velocity increases significantly from its slow-tempoed beginning. That is not at all to say that Clair de lune is therefore superior to Reverie as they stand shoulder to shoulder. Rather, it is a feat worth mentioning because it is perhaps easier to achieve that maintenance in a song like Reverie where the tempo remains slow throughout.

Rachmaninoff and Shostakovich are both Soviet-era Russian composers. It is difficult to find a piece of music more powerful than the opening of the first movement of Rachmaninoff's Piano Concerto No. 2 In C Minor.

Harry Gregson-Williams's Narnia Lullaby although brief in duration is one of the most strikingly beautiful and transcendent pieces of music ever composed and is often played on the duduk. Steve Jablsonky and James Newton Howard are known for their contributions to motion picture soundtracks like Hans Zimmer. Steve Jablonsky is best known for his work in the Transformers series, and James Newton Howard is known for his work in many films including The Hunger Games series and his collaboration with Hans Zimmer in the first two films of The Dark Knight trilogy. Nicholas Hooper, Patrick Doyle, and Alexandre Desplat all contributed to the Harry Potter series soundtrack. Howard Shore composed The Lord Of The Rings series soundtrack. Michael Giacchino's Married Life from Up is a beautiful song, and Bear McCreary did the music for The Walking Dead TV series.

Steve Vai is one of the greatest guitarists of all time and there are no tracks that can compare to For The Love Of God or Tender Surrender. The greatest metal band of all time is Slipknot. However, the greatest metal song of all time is Bleed by Meshuggah. The drums on that song are so difficult that the calm interlude in the middle was written just so that the drummer Tomas Haake could have a break and rest. The part that starts right after that is one of the greatest pieces of music ever written.

The two poems are Dylan Thomas's Do Not Go Gentle Into That Good Night and William Ernest Henley's Invictus. Do Not Go Gentle Into That Good Night gained additional popularity in the modern era due to its use in the movie Interstellar, and Invictus was Nelson Mandela's favorite poem. He cited its words as something that would give him strength during all of the years he was wrongfully imprisoned. The last recording is Gary Oldman's reading of the ending of Charles Dickens's A Tale Of Two Cities.
