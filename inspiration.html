Hans zimmer is the greatest composer of the modern era and one of the greatest composers of all time. The breadth and depth of his work is unrivaled having composed some of the most beautiful, powerful, and emotional music ever written in the soundtracks of scores of major motion pictures. Some of his best work comes from The Dark Knight trilogy, The Pirates Of The Caribbean series, Inception, and Interstellar amongst many other films. From The Dark Knight trilogy his best compositions include Corynorhinus, Myotis, Molossus, A Dark Knight, Agent Of Chaos, and Why Do We Fall. Marry Me, an unreleased track from Pirates Of The Caribbean: At World's End, is one of the most beautiful scores ever written. Other incredible songs he has composed include Time from Inception, First Steps, No Time For Caution, and Mountains from Interstellar, Final Ascent from No Time To Die, and A Way Of Life and A Small Measure Of Peace from The Last Samurai. Hans has been known to work with director Christopher Nolan including on some of the films previously mentioned. The directorial genius of Nolan combined with the musical genius of Hans creates an unfettered hybrid yielding some of the most profound cinematic experiences, and a better composer-director pair there probably never was. World renowned cellist Stjepan Hauser did an incredible cello rendition of his composition Now We Are Free from Gladiator out of which is delivered a whole new experience from that song. Hauser, one of the best cellists of the modern era, is known for both his interpretations of traditional classical cello songs and renditions of songs not originally made for the cello. It should be noted that Hans collaborated significantly with James Newton Howard in the first two films of The Dark Knight Trilogy, Klaus Badelt in the first Pirates of the Caribbean film and Gladiator, and Lisa Gerrard in Gladiator so the credit goes to them as well.

Frederic Chopin is one of the greatest classical piano composers of all time. It is not said the he is the greatest because the pursuit of such a choice proves to be untenable. How could it be said that Chopin is the greatest when there exists the works of Franz Liszt, Beethoven's forever immortal Moonlight Sonata, and so many others. To live without them would be a crime on its best day, and saying any single one of them is the greatest feels like a betrayal of the others. After ample time passes grappling with this futile and even impossible decision, the only moral conclusion that can be ascertained that does not diminish any one composer is that there is no greatest but rather a canvas of faces and names all contributing in the most profound way to the musical experience; to the pursuit of part of which makes being alive more than just existence. However, if an answer to that question were to be compelled, then certainly Chopin would be one of the strongest candidates if not the strongest. Other composers have written songs equally as beautiful and moving as Chopin and that statement can be made without hesitation. What could position Chopin as the greatest is the quantity of incredible songs he composed. He, like other composers but especially he, had this innate ability to write piano pieces that expressed the soul and heart of man; to take emotion and put it into music. And he did not just do this once or twice or a handful of times but rather did so time and time again. In that sense, Chopin and Hans Zimmer are descendants of the same because their genius is most readily apparent by observing that not just here and there but on countless occasions they composed some of the most remarkable music ever written. In the same way that it can be endlessly pondered how Cormac McCarthy put pen to paper for Blood Meridian, a plethora of time can be wasted attempting to comprehend how Hans or Chopin composed one of their masterpieces let alone all of them. Chopin's series of 21 nocturnes embody this most aptly. Nocturne 19 in E Minor and Nocturne 20 in C Sharp Minor stand out as two of the best amongst them all and are a baring of a part of the soul of humanity. Then in Nocturne 1 in B Flat Minor there is the way it goes from A, down to A flat, down to G flat, back up to A flat, and finally back up to A in measures 67 through 69. Moving on to Nocturne 4 in F Major there is the striking contrast between the peace and calm of the first half and the intensity and velocity of the second half. Nocturne 21 in C Minor posseses some of the same essence of Nocturnes 19 and 20, and then after all of that there are still 16 nocturnes remaining each of which is a standalone work of art worthy of admiration. This is in which lies the basis for their exceptionalism - there is not one single one of them that is lacking in some way; not one single one of them that is out of place. Together, they form an elegeant series of 21 compositions each with their own uniqueness, righteousness, and story to narrate. Even between the ones possessing similarities said qualities are maintained through their subtle differences. Chopin's nocturnes collectively stand out as one of the most remarkable works of art ever created and truly have no equal. Unfortunately, nocturnes 20 and 21 were not appreciated until after his death due to being published posthumously. This is especially regretful for his 20th nocturne as it is arguably his most famous and undoubtedly one of the most beautiful he ever composed. In addition to his nocturnes his work includes 27 preludes (one of them unfinished), 27 etudes, 3 piano sonatas, and 4 ballades. Rivaling the emotional depth of his nocturnes are three of his preludes: Prelude 4 in E Minor, Prelude 6 in B Minor, and Prelude 15 in D Flat Major known as Raindrop Prelude. It is difficult for words to suffice any worthy articulation regarding Prelude in E Minor, and by his own request Chopin had it played at his funeral. What makes Prelude in D Flat Major so remarkable is that the first part of the song, albeit nice, is actually rather unremarkable, and then out of nowhere in the 27th measure it suddenly pivots into one of the deepest pieces of music ever written marked by 7 calm yet heavy hits of A flat followed by the repetitive hitting of G sharp in conjunction with some lower octave 2-note chord progressions beginning with a strike of G sharp and C sharp one octave down. Both of these preludes are equivalent to Nocturnes 19 and 20 in that a written description of their sentiment is mostly intangible. The relationship that his 6th prelude has to his 4th and 15th preludes is akin to the relationship his 21st nocturne has to his 19th and 20th nocturnes in that although his 4th and 15th preludes are his best, his 6th prelude contains some of their same essence and deserves to be remarked upon as well. It too by his own request was played at his funeral along with the third movement of his Piano Sonata No. 2 in B Flat Minor more commonly known as Funeral March and parts of Mozart's Requiem. Then there are songs like Fantaisie Impromptu, Winter Wind, and Revolutionary Etude which capture an intensity and even at times a kind of violence that Chopin is capable of. Lastly, the beauty, power, emotion, and technical prowess of his first and most famous Ballade, Ballade No. 1 in G Minor, defensibly situates it as Chopin's crowning achievement. La Ballade as it became known as manifests all of Chopin's capabilities within one single song. With parts that are gentle and unhurried, parts that are swift and vehement, parts that are awe inspiring, and in its totality a traversal of much of the emotional range, Chopin's first ballade is demonstrative of his illustriousness and everything that he is capable of as a composer. Chopin died fairly young at the age of 39. Somewhere out there in the universe are all of the compositions he would have written yet to be heard or experienced like a truth that is yet to be known. Maybe one of them would have even been another Prelude in E Minor, Nocturne in C Sharp Minor, or Ballade No. 1 in G Major, and it is nothing short of a tragedy if that were true. However, it is inane to complain or lament about his premature death and what could have been. Chopin graced this world for some time and gifted it an abundance of incredible music before succumbing to the inevitable conclusion. The world should just be grateful that he existed at all and it can be left at that. It has been said that Mozart, Beethoven, and Chopin never died. They simply became music.

Franz Liszt, like Chopin, is known for his beautiful, elegant, and technically demanding piano pieces such as the Liebestraum No. 3, Romance "O Pourquoi Donc" in E Minor, Consolation No. 3, Un Sospiro, his piano arrangement of Paganini's violin composition La Campanella, and his piano arrangement of Schubert's Serenade, amongst others. If it were to be said that Chopin is the greatest classical piano composer of all time then Liszt is maybe the first to come to mind for which it would feel like a betrayal was being enacted upon him. The beauty and emotion of Liebestraum No. 3 has a uniqueness to it unmatched by any other piano song, and that within Romance "O Pourqoi Donc" in E Minor, Consolation No. 3, and Un Sospiro parallels Chopin's work. His piano arrangement of Schubert's Serenade is able to capture the same emotion and beauty of that song on the piano that exists in the arrangement on the strings. The emotion of the opening melody beginning with A, B flat, A, and D is only furthered by the intensity and almost unexpectedness of its shift one octave down partway through. La Campanella adequately illustrates Liszt's reputation for creating these technically demanding songs which in this case is partially due to the exhausting movement of the right hand constantly jumping back and forth to different octaves just to play consecutive notes, and its ending stands out as one of the most remarkable endings to any piano song.

Franz Schubert is most well known for Serenade but has other incredible compositions as well such as Impromptu in G Flat Major and Piano Trio No. 2 in E Flat Major 2nd Movement. Serenade was originally composed as a lied for voice and piano, but the most widely recognized version of it has the main melody played with strings, most commonly a cello. The cello and the piano ascend as some of the most beautiful instruments in all of music and are potentially the most direct gateways into the soul and heart of man as Chopin put it. In and of themselves they are beautiful, and together they create a type of musical duality, a distinctive fusion, that is born out in any composition derived from their pairing. Schubert unfortunately died even younger than Chopin at the age of 31.

Ludwig van Beethoven is most well known for his symphonies and his Piano Sonata No. 14 in C Sharp Minor colloquially known as Moonlight Sonata. The sentiment of Moonlight Sonata 1st Movement cannot be underscored enough and it is a timeless piece that exists in its own realm. Even if Beethoven had quite literally composed no other music he could still be thrusted into the hierarchy of the greats due to this piece's composition. Fewer know of Moonlight Sonata 3rd Movement which is regarded as one of the most technically challenging piano pieces ever. Like Chopin's La Ballade, Moonlight Sonata 3rd Movement revealed unseen heights, dimensions, and possibilities within the piano that were hidden from the world prior to its composition. Like some sadistic action partaken in the name of sickly jest, Beethoven was afflicted with progressive deafness later in his life. Despite this, not only was he was still able to compose music during this time but he composed Symphony No. 9 which along with Symphony No. 5 is regarded as his greatest. This feat is a testament to the eminence of his musical being and intuition and he was truly an unshakeable musical genius.

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart's best work includes Fantasia No. 3 in D Minor, Serenade for Winds, and The Requiem in D Minor. Mozart's style was dominated by the pursuit of musical exquisiteness and perfection less so than the expression of emotion like Chopin or Liszt. This is seldom not the case, if ever, when comparing composers from the Classical and Romantic periods of which Mozart was from the former and Chopin and Liszt the latter. Inevitably, many retain an involuntary partiality, even an incredibly strong one, but neither style could so boldly be claimed to be inherently superior or pure in its musical essence or a composer more of a genius by virtue thereof. Rather, they are the peaks of two separate mountains, and the superiority is derived from the preference of the individual. Despite his style, the lifeblood of the Romantic period did at times appear to exist within Mozart as can be heard in the first part of Fantasisa No. 3 in D Minor. The Requiem, regarded as Mozart's final masterpiece, is divided into 8 primary sections with 15 unique pieces in total. The most recognizable pieces include I. Introitus: Requiem aeternam, II. Sequentia: Dies irae, and III. Sequentia: Lacrimosa with Introitus opening with those ever recognizable hits of D, C sharp, D, E/A, F, A flat...feeling like a heavy sigh and its associated feelings encountered in a difficult time or a confrontation with death at the terminus of one's life. Mozart died before he could complete The Requiem, and as a result of this there is unfortunately much controversy surrounding the piece and the events leading up to his death. There is debate concerning which parts of The Requiem are his own work, and whether or not his death was the result of a rather sudden illness or a slow deterioration of his health in the months prior. It is also alleged that when he knew he was dying he professed that he believed he was writing The Requiem for himself but that is debated as well. Although it is all speculative, this alleged profession and these facts makes the piece all the more compelling because what is true is that Mozart was struck with an illness at some point during the piece's composition, and he ultimately died before its completion. So, in that sense he was writing The Requiem for himself. The point being, regardless of which parts of The Requiem truly are his own work, regardless of whether or not his illness came on suddenly or it was slow and progressive, and regardless of whether or not he actually uttered that statement or at a minimum believed it in his own conscience, the compellingness of the circumstances is not lessened by the ambiguity surrounding what is historically accurate. I fear that I am writing a Requiem for myself he is alleged to have said.

Johann Sebastian Bach's best work includes Toccata and Fugue in D Minor, Air on the G String, and his famous Prelude from Cello Suite No. 1 in G Major. The almost divine transcendence of Toccata and Fugue In D Minor separates it from every other piece ever written for the organ. Air on the G String, a modified arrangement of Bach's Orchestral Suite No. 3 in D Major 2nd Movement by August Wilhelmj, makes a laudable attempt to embody the music of the air most notably felt in those long, drawn out notes that hover gracefully in the atmosphere. Yo-Yo Ma most exceptionally describes Bach's famed Cello Prelude as a representation of the infinitude of the natural world be that the infinite beauty of nature or how it is constant and unchanging.

Sergei Rachmaninoff is one of the most famous Russian composers and lived both before and during the Soviet Union. A prevailing theme in much of his work is that his ability to create powerful music is juxtaposed against his ability to create beautiful music. It is difficult to find a piece of music more powerful than the opening of the first movement of Piano Concerto No. 2 in C Minor or the 4th movement of Moment Musicaux in E Minor which is comparable to Chopin's Revolutionary Etude. Then, compare those pieces to the 2nd movement of his piano concerto or the first movement of Morceaux de Fantaisie which strikes at the heart of some of Chopin's nocturnes. Some of his pieces, like the opening to his piano concerto just mentioned, coalesce power and beauty and are simultaneously beautifully powerful and powerfully beautiful. Thus, Rachmaninoff is a perfect example of the rare and impressive creative dichotomy that exists within certain musical geniuses.

Erik Satie composed a series of Gnossiennes and Gymnopedies with the first composition in each series being the best and most well known. There is a deep level of calmness within his first Gymnopedie, and his first Gnossienne is a hauntingly beautiful piece for which it is difficult to pinpoint which emotion it strikes at. The spirit of these compositions truly situates them uniquely amongst all other pieces of music ever composed. Satie's work positions him somewhat as the stereotypical mad scientist or crazed artist - in a good sense - attempting to go places no one else has traversed. His music, and especially his Gnossiennes, really isn't comparable to other compositions.

Claude Debussy's work has its own particular essence comprised of a beautiful tranquility embedded within its sound by which it be identified. Debussy's most recognizable song Clair de lune is similar to Beethoven's Moonlight Sonata 1st Movement or Bach's Cello Prelude in that it is a song most would recognize even if not attuned to classical music. Fewer have probably heard Reverie. The true magnificence of Clair de lune lies within the fact that Debussy is able to maintain his signature essence aforementioned even in the parts where the velocity increases significantly from its slow-tempoed beginning. That is not at all to say that Clair de lune is therefore superior to Reverie as they stand shoulder to shoulder. Rather, it is a feat worth mentioning because it is perhaps easier to achieve that maintenance in a song like Reverie where the tempo remains slow throughout. Arabesque No. 1 is like the flow of water or the breath of life as it cascades from E and F sharp down to a lower octave B in its most recognizable part.

Gabriel Faure is most well known for Elegie Op. 24. Arranged for the cello and piano, it is once again an embodiment of the incredible beauty that materializes when the cello and piano come together and is one of the most emotionally intense and beautiful pieces of music ever written. Elegie is ultimately about death and Faure wrote it for his wife after her passing. The beginning of the song is constructed by utilizing sets of threes. It opens with C minor playing in forte the most iconic part of its beautifully sad and introspective melody that makes the journey from E flat down to C one octave below striking the soul. This is played for a second time pianissimo in the 6th measure and for a third time in the 18th measure pianississimo. Along the way in the 10th measure right after the second occurence of the opening melody there begins a passionately intense build up to three sets of F and G sharp in the 16th and 17th measures consisting of three sets of two heavy hits of the same note starting at A, then C, and then A again one octave above. It is right after reaching this peak at F Sharp and G that it abruptly reverts back to the third occurence of the opening melody. The disparity between the intensity of the peak and the quiet softness of that third occurence is undescribably compelling and moving and one of the most beautiful parts of the song. The song then transitions to something hopeful and uplifting in measures 23 through 34 immediately followed by a section whose intensity is marked by power, velocity, and anger. Then at measure 39 this intensity is passed on to the opening melody foisting onto it a whole new shade of meaning. After its completion in measure 42 there is a brief section where the piano takes charge with the cello holding a quiet yet resolute C in the background. The focus is then returned to the cello in measure 47 where the song continues to live out the rest of its life terminating in that same C. All of these parts contribute to Elegie being one of the most beautiful songs ever written on the cello, one of the most beautiful songs ever written in general, and nothing less than a complete musical masterpiece.

Sir Edward William Elgar is most well known for his Cello Concerto in E Minor which rests squarely adjacent to Faure's Elegie. Both exude many of the same emotions including a sense of finality. As previously mentioned, Faure composed it to describe the ultimate finality, and in Elgar's case it was the final piece he ever composed. It is inconceivable that the concerto did not initially receive the recognition and praise that it deserves. A 1965 recording by cellist Jacqeuline de Pru catalyzed the development of its reverence and at that point decades had passed since its debut. Elgar identified with this particular piece of music more than any he ever wrote, and shortly before his death he remarked that after his passing if someone is ever heard whistling this song up in the Malvern Hills that it would be him. Even though he did not die until 15 years after its composition, the song feels like his farewell to life due to the combination of its emotion and it being the last piece he ever composed. The first movement of Elgar's concerto and Faure's Elegie exemplify the cello's ability to be a distinctive conduit for emotion, and they are some of the most emotionally powerful cello scores ever composed.

Camille Saint-Saens is most well known for his piece The Swan from The Carnival of the Animals and his Cello Concerto in A Minor whose beginning and ending constitute the most recognizable parts. The Swan is traditionally played by a cello accompanied by a piano or a harp. Neither version is superior but rather they are just different. Thus, The Swan becomes even further remarkable because it is a single song out of which comes two different experiences which are each uniquely beautiful in their own way. Like some of the compositions formerly mentioned, the piano-cello version of The Swan captures that unrivaled duality that exists with the piano and the cello.

Remo Giazotto was a 20th century musicologist known only for his song Adagio in G Minor. Adagio's composition is often mistakenly credited to Tomaso Albinoni of whom Giazotto was a biographer and as a result it is referred to as Albinoni's Adagio. Giazotto originally claimed that a small amount of the song came from Albinoni but later retracted that claim so the facts remain unclear. However, the accepted narrative is that the credit for the majority of the song goes to Giazotto even if a small amount did come from Albinoni. If Giazotto truly did compose the song and not Albinoni, then his talent as a composer was largely wasted given that it is the only song he ever composed yet is also one of the most beautiful songs ever composed. Beginning with a sublime 8 measure introduction most breathtakingly played on the organ, the song then transitions into the melody with much of it marked by the repetitive plucking of some background strings serving as its figurative heartbeat, propelling forward even further the heavy beauty of the melody.

Samuel Barber is one of the most acclaimed American composers of the 20th century most known for his song Adagio for Strings originally arranged for a string orchestra but also commonly performed with a choir in which it is alternatively known as Agnus Dei. Although the strings arrangement is nice, the unchallengable sound and emotion of the cello really drives forward the cello arrangement to be the best of them all.

Dmitri Shostakovich was a Russian composer like Rachmaninoff. His most famous composition Waltz No. 2 unexpectedly integrates the saxophone into an otherwise traditionally classical song which delivers a pleasant and soothing surprise. Although it is also commonly played on the cello, and versions that do this deserve recognition, its grandest form remains with the saxophone and as such it is a truly unique piece of music.

Modest Mussorgsky, another Russian Composer worth mentioning, composed a number of great compositions including the second movement vecchio castello of his composition Pictures at an Exhibition. Upon realizing that vecchio castello translates to old castle, the eerily beautiful feeling of the song becomes quite fitting.

Max Bruch's Kol Nidre and Franz Gruber's Silent Night are two incredible compositions coming from the Jewish and Christian traditions. Although not very comparable in their actual sentiment they are comparable in their beauty.

Harry Gregson-Williams's Narnia Lullaby, although brief in duration, is one of the most strikingly beautiful and transcendent pieces of music ever composed and is often played on the duduk. Steve Jablsonky and James Newton Howard are known for their contributions to motion picture soundtracks like Hans Zimmer. Steve Jablonsky is best known for his work in the Transformers series, and James Newton Howard is known for his work in many films including The Hunger Games series and his collaboration with Hans Zimmer in the first two films of The Dark Knight trilogy. Nicholas Hooper, Patrick Doyle, and Alexandre Desplat all contributed to the Harry Potter series soundtrack. Howard Shore composed The Lord Of The Rings series soundtrack. Michael Giacchino's Married Life from Up is a beautiful song, and Bear McCreary did the music for The Walking Dead TV series.

Steve Vai is one of the greatest guitarists of all time and there are no tracks that can compare to For The Love Of God and Tender Surrender. The greatest metal band of all time is Slipknot. However, the greatest metal song of all time is Bleed by Meshuggah. The drums on that song are so difficult that the calm interlude in the middle was written just so that the drummer Tomas Haake could have a break and rest. The part that starts right after that is one of the greatest pieces of music ever written.

It is difficult to make assertions wholly lacking uncertainty. Some philosohpers would even say the claim that two plus two equals four is still based on some a priori assumption about the nature of reality. But if the task were to be taken on then it could be asserted that music is the greatest tool humans have at their disposal for the expression of emotion, experience, and the human condition. That is not at all to say that other tools such as the spoken or written word are inferior or lacking in some sense. It is impossible to read Carton describe the far, far better thing that he does than he has ever done and the far, far better rest that he goes to than he has ever known, and not feel something; to read Sproule tell The Kid that he knows his kind and what's wrong with him is wrong all the way through him, and not feel a chill. There are times where words can suffice and can even suffice better than anything else. Everything has its place. But there are also times where they cannot. Music is a conduit for the soul in these times - it has the ability to at times describe what cannot be described, to narrate what cannot be narrated, and to express what cannot be expressed in any other form. And although all genres of music can be equally powerful, the time does need to be taken, without reservation, to single out classical music. Many traverse the circle never making this discovery despite it being in plain view. The witholding of that discovery, the deprivation of that experience, is one of the greatest tragedies of all. But if one does make that discovery, if one does surmount that peak, then it can be heard and felt all of that which Chopin, Liszt, Schubert, Beethoven, Mozart, Bach, Rachmaninoff, Satie, Debussy, Faure, Elgar, Saint-Saens and all the rest of them were trying to say. One simply needs to listen.
